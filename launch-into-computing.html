Launch into Computing

Foundation module — April 2026 (Intake A), 12 weeks, 20 credits.
Units 1–9 worked through — from logic and algorithms to AI, cybersecurity, and disaster recovery.

Module status
Active module — updated progressively as the 12-week module runs. Done: formative activities for Units 1–9, the graded Collaborative Learning Discussion (drafted), the reflective piece, the skills matrix, and the action plan. Pending: tutor and peer feedback on the discussion (added verbatim when received), and Units 10–12 as they open.

Learning outcomes

On completion of this module, students will be able to:

  1. Identify and critically analyse computing challenges and processes in business systems, accounting for the current enterprise landscape.
  2. Gather and synthesise information from multiple sources (including internet resources and business publications) to aid in the systematic design and analysis of computing challenges.
  3. Critically evaluate appropriate methodologies, tools and techniques to mitigate and/or solve computing issues and their business impact.
  4. Articulate the legal, social, ethical, and professional issues faced by computing professionals.

Artefacts

This module is assessed by a single Collaborative Learning Discussion (20%) spanning Units 1–4. The per-unit tasks beneath it are formative activities that build the evidence base across software engineering, AI, cybersecurity, data science, and enterprise IT.

Graded assessment — Collaborative Learning Discussion (Units 1–4)

AI in Healthcare — a four-part contribution (~1,301 words): an Initial Post on automated retinal-image analysis for diabetic retinopathy screening in NHS England, two Peer Responses (cybersecurity in financial services; generative AI for clinical documentation), and a reflective Summary Post. It argues that computing’s value is conditional on governance, transparency, and accountability rather than on capability alone.

  • Demonstrates: LO1–LO4 — critical analysis, multi-source synthesis, and legal / ethical framing.
  • References: six Cite Them Right Harvard sources (Topol 2019; Rudnicka et al. 2025; Obermeyer et al. 2019; Gebremeskel and Romeo 2025; ENISA 2023; Apruzzese et al. 2023).
  • Source: Unit 1/assignment/Collaborative Learning Discussion - AI in Healthcare.docx. Status: drafted across four components; contributing to the module forum. Feedback: to be quoted verbatim once received.

Formative activities (Units 2–9)

Unit 2 — Logical foundations: Boolean algebra & logic circuits. A reflective report on Boolean logic in modern computing (grounded in Boole, 1854 and Shannon, 1938) plus the design of a three-input majority-voter circuit: Boolean derivation Y = AB + AC + BC, a full truth table, and a gate-level circuit diagram. Demonstrates LO1 / LO3 — formal reasoning and digital logic.

Source: Unit 2/Unit 2 Logic Gates and Set Theory Tasks Final.docx · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 3 — Algorithm analysis: bubble sort vs quicksort. A runnable Python benchmark comparing the two sorts on seeded inputs (correctness-checked against sorted(), best-of-five timing), with linear- and log-scale performance graphs and a 300-word Big-O analysis. Demonstrates LO3 — computational complexity and empirical evaluation.

Source: Unit 3/assignment/bubble_quick.py · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 4 — Comparative analysis of development methodologies. A 500-word report comparing Waterfall and Agile on flexibility, project-size suitability, and risk / quality management, recommending one per scenario with academic and industry support. Demonstrates LO3 — methodology evaluation.

Source: Unit 4/assignment/Comparative Analysis of Software Development Methodologies.docx · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 5 — Data science and storage. Two strands: a Netflix case-study forum response on data science, SQL-vs-NoSQL fit and the data-governance trade-off (with peer replies); and a Python data-analytics task on the OWID COVID-19 dataset — cleaned per-million series for five countries, descriptive statistics, and a best-lag case-to-death Pearson correlation, output as a summary table and trend charts. Demonstrates LO1 / LO2 / LO4 — data analysis, storage models, and data ethics.

Source: Unit 5/assignment/data_analytics_covid.py · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 6 — Implementing a simple AI model. A Naive Bayes SMS spam classifier (scikit-learn CountVectorizer + MultinomialNB) trained on the UCI SMS Spam Collection with a stratified split, scored against a keyword-rule baseline and reported with a confusion matrix (98.7% accuracy, 92.0% recall, 95.1% F1) and a 290-word analysis covering metrics and bias. Demonstrates LO3 / LO4 — ML implementation and evaluation.

Source: Unit 6/assignment/spam_classifier.py · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 7 — Security risk assessment. A 500-word, five-section assessment of an LLM-based customer-support assistant at a financial-services firm: threats (prompt injection, personal-data exposure, phishing, insider misuse) rated by impact and likelihood, with mitigations (MFA + least-privilege RBAC, model safeguards, encryption), referenced to the ICO, the NIST AI RMF, and the OWASP LLM Top 10. Demonstrates LO3 / LO4 — AI threat modelling.

Source: Unit 7/assignment/Security Risk Assessment Report - AI Customer Support Assistant.docx · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 8 — Cybersecurity threat assessment & mitigation plan. A 500-word, five-section report on a UK private healthcare provider facing ransomware via phishing with cloud-hosted patient records: threats rated by impact and likelihood, three layered mitigations (identity-centred access control, AES-256 / TLS encryption, resilience and governance via NIST CSF 2.0 and ISO/IEC 27001), and the ransom-payment dilemma anchored to the ICO / NCSC position. Demonstrates LO3 / LO4 — risk management and GDPR / ISO compliance.

Source: Unit 8/assignment/Cybersecurity Threat Assessment and Mitigation Plan - UK Healthcare Provider.docx · Status: drafted (formative practice).

Unit 9 — Disaster recovery plan. A 500-word DRP for a cloud-based e-commerce retailer: risks ranked by likelihood and impact, a Recovery Time Objective of one hour and Recovery Point Objective of five minutes, 3-2-1 immutable backups (Bacula), multi-region failover (AWS Elastic Disaster Recovery), checksum integrity verification, and a legal / ethical / professional reflection against UK GDPR and ISO 22301. Demonstrates LO3 / LO4 — business continuity and resilience.

Source: Unit 9/assignment/Disaster Recovery Plan - Cloud E-Commerce Platform.docx · Status: drafted (formative practice).

The activities above are formative practice; the graded outcome is the Collaborative Learning Discussion. Source files are named for reference and held in the module workspace, not in this public repository.

Reflections

Using the 3 W's framework prescribed by the e-Portfolio brief (alternative: Gibbs' Reflective Cycle).

What? — description

During Unit 1 I produced a four-part discussion-forum contribution on artificial intelligence in healthcare: an Initial Post on automated retinal-image analysis for diabetic retinopathy screening in NHS England, two Peer Responses (one on cybersecurity in financial services, one on generative AI for clinical documentation), and a Summary Post synthesising the discussion. I cited six sources across the four components, used Cite Them Right Harvard formatting throughout, and wrote in British English. The exercise was the first formal academic engagement of this MSc and my first time writing in this discussion-and-synthesis format.

So what? — interpretation

The most significant thing I learnt was that my thinking on computing's role in industry shifted noticeably through the discussion. I came in framing AI in healthcare primarily as an operational efficiency tool — faster screening, better resource allocation, reduced clinician workload. Engaging with classmates' posts on cybersecurity (Maria) and generative-AI clinical documentation (Donald) made it clear that the same pattern recurs across sectors: each technology promises scale and speed, and each carries proportional governance, accountability, and human-oversight costs. By the time I wrote the Summary Post my framing had shifted to: computing's value is conditional on how systems are governed and managed in practice, not on the capability of the technology itself.

This shift mattered because, as a Senior Software Engineer working on healthcare systems for the Western Cape Department of Health, I have first-hand professional exposure to the governance side of these tradeoffs — but my prior framing of the work was largely engineering-pragmatic rather than conceptually structured. The academic lens, particularly Obermeyer et al.'s (2019) bias work and Apruzzese et al.'s (2023) critique of over-reliance on automation, gave me language and citations to articulate concerns I had previously held intuitively. It also reframed my sense of where the most important software-engineering decisions in healthcare AI sit: not at the model-development layer alone but in the governance, monitoring, and review processes around it.

What next? — outcome

Two things follow concretely. First, in the remaining modules of the MSc — particularly Understanding Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning — I will hold my reading to the test of asking whether the methods presented account for governance and accountability assumptions, or whether they assume those concerns away. Second, in my professional practice I intend to be more deliberate when challenging vendor claims for AI-based features in healthcare systems: rather than focusing on technical capability alone, I will ask for evidence on bias evaluation, drift monitoring, and clear accountability for AI-influenced decisions. The Unit 1 exercise has confirmed that the academic and professional sides of this MSc reinforce each other — the academic framing is sharpening intuitions I have built through practice, and the practice is giving the academic concepts a concrete environment to apply to.

Module synthesis — Units 2–9

Working through the technical units reinforced and complicated the Unit 1 insight. The logic, algorithms, and software-engineering tasks (Units 2–4) grounded practice-based intuitions in formal foundations: deriving a majority-voter circuit and analysing sorting complexity made explicit the reasoning I usually apply tacitly. The data-science and AI units (Units 5–6) turned the governance argument into something measurable — building and evaluating a spam classifier showed how accuracy, precision, and recall trade off, and why a single headline metric can hide a costly error class. The security and risk units (Units 7–9) closed the loop: assessing AI-system threats, a ransomware scenario, and a disaster-recovery plan made clear that the governance, accountability, and resilience concerns I first raised about healthcare AI recur in every computing discipline. Across the module my framing moved from technology-as-capability to systems-as-governed, and I now read each new method by asking what it assumes about oversight, failure, and recovery.

Reflective piece across Units 1–9; to be revised against the brief’s Distinction criteria before module-end submission, and extended as Units 10–12 open.
